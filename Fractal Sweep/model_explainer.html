Fractal Sweep — How the Model Works

A plain-English walkthrough, with diagrams, of what the engine looks for and why each filter exists.

One-sentence summary. Fractal Sweep watches for a specific 3-stage pattern (sweep → return → confirmation) on 12 years of NQ futures data, scores each trade as a clean win or loss at 1R, then layers five optional filters that you can toggle live in the dashboard to find the highest-edge combination.

The three-stage pattern

Every Fractal Sweep setup unfolds in three stages, all of which must happen inside the same higher-timeframe (HTF) candle's window. If any stage doesn't complete before the next HTF candle opens, the setup is discarded.

Stage 1
Sweep — grabbing liquidity
On the HTF chart (1-hour or 30-minute), a candle wicks above the prior candle's high (or below its low). The price that gets touched is called the sweep_extreme.

This is liquidity-grab behavior: stop-losses and buy-stops sit just above old highs, so a quick wick takes them out. The setup is short-biased if the high was swept, long-biased if the low was swept.

The sweep can happen at any minute within the HTF candle — the engine records exactly when.
Stage 2
Return — back inside the range
After the sweep, price has to come back inside the prior candle's range. The first bar that closes back inside is the "return bar."

Why this matters: if price keeps running past the swept level, the sweep was real momentum — not a fake-out. Coming back inside is the first sign of rejection.

Without a return, no trade. The setup is dead.
Stage 3
CISD — change in state of delivery
On the lower timeframe (5-minute or 3-minute), the engine walks backward from the return bar and finds the run of opposing candles that built the move up. The cisd_level is the open of the earliest candle in that run.

The CISD fires when a single new candle closes back through that level — for a short setup, a candle closes below cisd_level. That close is the confirmation that delivery has switched direction.

Entry = open of the next bar after CISD fires. Stop = the original sweep extreme. Target = 1R.
Stage 4
Outcome — 1R or stopped out
Once the trade is live, the engine walks forward 1-minute bars and tracks whichever level price hits first.

Stop loss: the original sweep extreme. If price comes back to take it out, the trade loses 1R.
Target: 1R measured from entry. If price reaches it, the trade wins 1R.
Tie-break: if the bar's high-low range covers both stop and target, the stop wins (the engine matches the Pine indicator's intrabar tie behavior).

Every trade is a clean +1R or −1R, which makes win-rate the headline metric.

Two timeframe pairs

Fractal Sweep runs the same pattern detection across two timeframe combinations — each is a separate model with its own stats. Both default to the same risk profile (1R target).

1H_5M
Sweep happens on the 1-hour candle. CISD confirmation is read on the 5-minute chart. Slower, ~140 trades/year. Cleaner signal, larger swings.
30M_3M
Sweep on the 30-minute candle, CISD on 3-minute. ~270 trades/year — roughly double the frequency. More noise, but more setups to filter.

The five filters

The model has no meaningful edge on its own — baseline win-rate is around 50%, which is the trade tax (commissions, slippage) territory. The edge comes from filters. Each filter is optional, toggleable live in the dashboard, and adds a requirement on top of the base 3-stage pattern.

F3
Shallow Sweep
The sweep wick has to be 50% or less of the prior candle's full range. Tickle the level, don't punch through it.
+3.4% WR · +0.061R EV
F4
Closed Back Inside
The return bar's close must sit back inside the prior HTF range — not just the wick.
noise alone · useful in combos
SMT
NQ-ES Divergence
NQ swept its level but ES did not sweep its corresponding level. Structural disagreement = stronger signal.
+7.8% WR · +0.150R EV (strongest)
P42
Late Extreme (:42)
The prior HTF candle's swept extreme had to form in the last 30% of that candle's window — past minute :42 on H1, minute :21 on 30M. From Amas's "magic number."
+1.7% over SMT (1H) · marginal alone
PD
CISD in PD
For shorts, the CISD bar's close must sit in the premium half of the prior HTF range (≥ 50%). For longs, the discount half (≤ 50%). From Fearing's "premium CISD" thesis.
+3.2% (1H) / +3.7% (30M) over SMT

Why filters matter so much. Without filters, baseline WR sits around 50% — no edge. Stacked: F3 ∧ F4 ∧ SMT reaches ~59% WR. Add the new Phase-1 filters (+ PD) and the best combo climbs to ~63% WR on the 1H model. The dashboard's Filters tab enumerates all 32 combinations so you can see which mix has the highest EV for any timeframe pair.

How is this different from NPG Sweep?

Both engines watch a similar high-level pattern (sweep → return → confirmation) on the same database. The differences are in the details.

AspectFractal SweepNPG Sweep
Setup detection
Sweep definition Sweep + return-to-range. F4 filter enforces the close-back-inside check explicitly. Wick Lick: same shape, with an explicit double-sweep exclusion (candles touching both extremes are skipped).
CISD definition Single-bar engulf. One close past the cisd_level fires the trade. Series-based. Walks back, builds an opposing-candle series (up to 20 bars), fires only when a close breaks the entire series.

Target 1R, 100% exit at target. Clean win/loss. Four levels at 0.5×, 1.0×, 1.5×, 2.0× of series range. 25% scale-out at each.
Composite outcome Always exactly +1R or −1R. Weighted sum — partial wins are possible if TPs hit before the stop.

Built-in F3, F4, SMT, P42, PD — five filters, 32 combinations. SMT, Silver (late-week + aggressive close).

Glossary

Sweep
When a candle's high or low pokes past a prior candle's extreme. The "sweep extreme" is the actual price reached — typically where stop-losses live.
Return bar
The first bar after the sweep whose close comes back inside the prior candle's range. Marks the start of the rejection.
CISD
Change in State of Delivery. The cisd_level is the open of the earliest candle in the run that built the swept extreme; CISD "fires" when a close breaks that level.
1R
One unit of risk = absolute distance from entry to stop. A trade that wins exactly its risk distance = +1R. Stopped out = −1R.
EV
Expected value, in R-units per trade. EV = WR × +1 + (1 − WR) × −1 for the simple 1R profile. A coin-flip is 0R EV; +0.10R EV is a real edge.
PF
Profit factor. Sum of winning R-units divided by sum of losing R-units. A PF of 1.50 means winners earn 1.5× the size of losers (assuming equal counts).
SMT
Smart Money Tool — divergence between NQ and ES. If NQ sweeps but ES doesn't, it's an SMT — historically the strongest single edge in the model.
Anchor window
The time bucket of the HTF candle (1 hour or 30 minutes). Sweep, return, and CISD must all happen inside one window. Setups that bleed past the next HTF candle are discarded.
